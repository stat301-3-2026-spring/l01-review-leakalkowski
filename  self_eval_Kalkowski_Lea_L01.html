Question 2

Explain the difference between supervised and unsupervised learning.

TipSolution

In unsupervised learning, we don’t have a target of interest. We don’t have an outcome that supervises the process. It is a data-driven model. Objective is pattern/structure recognition.

In supervised learning, there is a target of interest/outcome variable so it is a task driven model. Objective is predictions and predicitve models.

Part (f)

Describe the bias-variance trade-off when considering the use of a mechanistic or empirically driven model.

TipSolution

Bias is the error of being too simple, more along the lines of underfitting. Variance is the error from being too sensitive, so like overfitting. There is a bias variance trade off when you make your model more or less complex. As the flexibility of your model increases, bias decreases but variance increases.

When using a mechanistic model, bias is typically higher and the variance is lower. If the true relationship doesn’t necessarily match your assumptions, then your model will miss patterns. But, small changes in the data won’t completely change the model.

For empirically driven models, bias is lower meaning that it can approximate the true function fairly well, but variance is high and more susceptible to noise.

Question 6

When splitting the data, why is it useful to stratify by the outcome/target variable?

TipSolution

Splitting by the outcome/target variable ensures that there is a relatively even distribution of that variable within each set. If you didn’t have an even distribution, then your model would be trained on different distributions which can be a problem for predictions.

Question 7

Briefly describe how v-fold cross validation with repeats is used to estimate test RMSE. Also provide an explanation of why we use it.

TipSolution

The training data is split into v number of roughly even folds. Common v numbers are 3, 5, and 10. Then for each fold, the model is trained with the other v-1 folds, predicts the left-out fold, then computes the RMSE. This process is repeated for all folds, then the RMSE is averaged. That process repeats for how ever many repeats you tell your model to use, with the average of all RMSE’s across all the folds and repeats being calculated.

It’s helpful when there are not that many observations. There are more models created, which means more accurate averages and variance is reduces.

Question 8

When might we use a bootstrap resampling procedure instead of v-fold cross validation to estimate test RMSE?

TipSolution

Bootstrap is preferred when the dataset is very small. For v-folds, splitting the dataset into mutually exclusive folds can make the training sets small. With bootstrap, replacing lets you use observations multiple time. Additionally, bootstrap gives you a distribution of RMSE if you want a measure of uncertainty.

Question 9

Briefly describe model tuning and why we use it.

TipSolution

Tuning is when you adjust the hyperparameters of your model. Instead of setting hyperparameters on your own and guessing, like the number of neighbors for KNN, you adjust and test a range of them and are told the best ones. This is finding the “sweet spot” of all your model’s different hyperparameters and combinations of them.
